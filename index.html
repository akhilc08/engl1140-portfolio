Introduction
What This Course Asked of Me

This portfolio collects the reading and writing I did in Writing Medicine: Stories of Illness and Healing. The course moved across novels, short stories, film, and journalism, all asking one question in different registers: what does suffering do to people, and what do people do with suffering?

The works I read (Lu Xun, Michael Ondaatje, Yu Hua, Toni Morrison, Kazuo Ishiguro) each approached that question differently. What surprised me was how often the answer wasn't about healing at all. It was about what breaks, what persists, and what belief alone cannot fix.

Three essays follow. Each one is wrong in some small way I can still see. That's what writing is.

Closing
What I Carry Out

I came into this course thinking literature about illness was about the body. I was wrong. It is about what the body reveals: the beliefs that outlast evidence, the identities that war strips away, the systems that outlive any single person's attempt to escape them.

The best writing in this course (Lu Xun's flat irony, Ondaatje's lyric grief, Yu Hua's blunt accumulation of loss) didn't console. It clarified. It named what was happening at the scale where names matter most.

I want to write like that someday.
